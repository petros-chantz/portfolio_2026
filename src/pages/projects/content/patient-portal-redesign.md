## Context
A regional healthcare provider had been running a legacy patient portal built in the early 2010s. Patients used it primarily to book appointments and view test results, but abandonment rates were high and the support team was handling thousands of avoidable calls per month.

## Problem
The portal had accumulated years of incremental additions without a coherent design strategy. Navigation was fragmented, terminology was clinical rather than patient-friendly, and the system was inaccessible to users relying on assistive technology.

## Role
I led design from early research through to handoff, facilitating co-design sessions with patients and clinical staff, and working closely with engineering to ensure accessibility requirements were met at component level.

## Process and decisions
We began with contextual interviews across three patient cohorts: people managing chronic conditions, caregivers scheduling on behalf of family members, and patients using assistive technology. Each group had fundamentally different mental models and failure points.

```block
{"type":"quote","text":"I just need to know when my appointment is and what I need to bring. Everything else gets in the way.","attribution":"Patient, discovery interview — chronic care cohort"}
```

From these sessions, three design principles emerged:
- Surface the right information at the right moment, not everything at once
- Write for a patient, not a clinician
- Treat accessibility as a constraint, not a feature

## Solution
The redesigned portal introduced a task-oriented home screen that surfaced upcoming appointments, pending actions, and new results without requiring navigation. Booking flows were restructured around patient intent rather than system architecture, and all copy was rewritten to plain language standards.

## Outcome and impact
Support call volume for portal-related queries dropped by 38% in the first quarter after beta launch. Task completion rates for appointment booking improved from 54% to 89% in unmoderated usability testing. The portal passed a third-party WCAG 2.1 AA audit with no critical failures.

## Reflection
Healthcare UX is unforgiving because the stakes are real. The most important design decision we made was slowing down the process to understand who was actually using the product, not who the organisation assumed was using it.

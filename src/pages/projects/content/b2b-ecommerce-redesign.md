## Context
A European industrial supplier wanted to move more sales online, but the catalogue had over 80,000 SKUs, complex pricing tiers, and ordering logic tightly coupled to a legacy ERP.

## Problem
Their first e-commerce attempt had failed because customers did not trust it. Pricing tiers appeared late, stock data felt unreliable, and taxonomy reflected ERP internals rather than customer mental models.

## Role
I led interaction architecture and purchasing-flow design, collaborating with commerce, ERP integration, and sales stakeholders to align the experience with real ordering behavior.

## Process and decisions
We mapped customer decisions around application context first, then linked those paths back to ERP part numbers behind the scenes. Pricing transparency moved earlier, and stock language became explicit and honest instead of binary.

## Solution
The redesigned product detail and listing flow surfaced price breaks, lead times, and procurement constraints before basket commitment.

## Outcome and impact
Within six months of launch, direct digital orders grew from 9% to 41% of volume, while inbound sales calls dropped by 28% and shifted toward higher-value advisory conversations.

## Reflection
In B2B contexts, trust is often won through transparency rather than simplification. Revealing operational constraints clearly outperformed attempts to hide complexity.
